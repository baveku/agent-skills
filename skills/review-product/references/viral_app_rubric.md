# Viral App Review Rubric

Use this reference when reviewing consumer apps, especially paid subscription utilities, AI apps, health apps, education apps, creative tools, productivity apps, women-focused apps, wellness apps, and micro-finance apps.

## Core Thesis

A viral consumer app wins by doing one painful job with unusually low friction, communicating the dream instantly, proving people pay, and distributing faster than competitors can react.

## Scoring Guide

### One-Job Clarity

High score:
- One primary job is obvious.
- Adjacent features do not dilute the wedge.
- The product can be described in one sentence by a normal user.

Low score:
- The app bundles many unrelated promises.
- The core use case is a category label, not a specific pain.
- The product looks like a generic feature set.

### Magic Moment

High score:
- One core interaction feels surprisingly easy or powerful.
- The user can show the moment in a short video.
- The moment naturally creates word of mouth.

Low score:
- The product only feels competent.
- The impressive part is internal technology, not user-perceived output.
- Sharing requires explanation.

### Friction Removal

High score:
- The app deletes the most annoying step in the old behavior.
- The first useful result arrives quickly.
- Inputs, permissions, and setup are aggressively minimized.

Low score:
- The user still does the hard work manually.
- The app adds tracking, forms, choices, or setup before proving value.
- Automation exists but does not remove the painful step.

### Dream, Not Features

High score:
- The offer sells a desired identity, outcome, saved time, better health, more money, or less pain.
- Features are framed as proof of the dream.
- Messaging uses user language rather than product language.

Low score:
- The copy leads with tools, tabs, models, databases, or features.
- The user cannot tell what life improvement they are buying.
- The promise is accurate but emotionally flat.

### 5-Second Comprehension

High score:
- A cold user can understand who it is for and why it matters in 5 seconds.
- The App Store listing, first screen, ad, or landing page makes the same promise.
- Visual proof supports the claim immediately.

Low score:
- The app needs a long explanation.
- Screens are polished but generic.
- Headlines are clever instead of clear.

### Paywall and Onboarding

High score:
- Paywall is designed before minor in-app polish.
- Onboarding increases purchase intent by amplifying pain, desire, and trust.
- The paywall offer is specific, credible, and outcome-led.
- Price, trial, guarantee, plan hierarchy, and CTA are clear.

Low score:
- Paywall is treated as a final screen after a neutral survey.
- Onboarding is short but fails to sell.
- The user reaches payment without understanding why now.

### Paid Demand

High score:
- The team is testing payment early.
- Users pay before the team overbuilds retention and polish.
- Free usage metrics are not mistaken for product-market fit.

Low score:
- Downloads, waitlist joins, or social likes are treated as proof.
- Payment is delayed until after many features ship.
- The app cannot articulate target CAC, price, and payback logic.

### Acquisition Economics

High score:
- The app has a realistic route to profitable acquisition.
- CAC can be tested through creator posts, paid ads, ASO, Apple Search Ads, or manual outreach.
- The team knows whether the funnel breaks before install or after paywall.

Low score:
- Growth depends on buying cheap downloads.
- CAC exceeds customer value.
- No one owns distribution.

### Market Tide and Social Validation

High score:
- The niche has active creator supply and visible buyer interest on TikTok, Instagram, YouTube Shorts, Reddit, or similar channels.
- Multiple creators can tell the story in distinct ways.
- The market is expanding, not stagnant.

Low score:
- Only one creator or one angle dominates the niche.
- Search interest exists but social proof is thin.
- The product fights against user behavior instead of riding it.

### Speed and Operating Cadence

High score:
- The team ships, tests, and iterates in days.
- Marketing tests start while the product is being built.
- Decision-making is flat enough that good ideas can move immediately.

Low score:
- The team waits for perfect launch quality.
- Roadmaps are comfortable rather than forcing pace.
- Approval chains slow obvious tests.

## Stage-Based Priorities

### Idea or Prototype

Focus on:
- One job.
- Dream promise.
- Social niche validation.
- Waitlist and creator angle tests.
- Mock paywall or preorder-style payment test.

Do not focus on:
- Full retention system.
- Extensive settings.
- Broad feature roadmap.

### Early Launch

Focus on:
- View-to-install ratio.
- Paywall reach.
- Paywall conversion.
- Onboarding as a sales flow.
- Manual creator/outreach loops.

Do not focus on:
- Perfect in-app polish.
- Complex analytics beyond the funnel.
- Large brand campaigns.

### Scaling

Focus on:
- CAC vs. LTV.
- Repeated winning angles.
- Creator volume.
- Paywall tests.
- App Store listing tests.
- Retention once acquisition and payment work.

Do not focus on:
- Novel hacks that efficient markets have already rejected.
- Agency complexity for simple channels.

## Diagnostic Shortcuts

- If people view but do not install, sharpen the dream, proof, category, audience, or visual demonstration.
- If people install but do not hit paywall, remove onboarding friction or make early steps more emotionally relevant.
- If people reach paywall but do not pay, improve pain framing, proof, price, offer clarity, trial mechanics, or trust.
- If people pay but churn quickly, the promise may be bigger than the delivered first value.
- If acquisition is unprofitable, reduce CAC, raise conversion, raise price, or narrow to a higher-intent niche.

## Review Tone

Be direct, commercial, and practical. Praise only what helps the app get users, payment, or repeatable distribution. Make the output useful for a founder deciding what to change this week.
